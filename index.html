Vought

During World War II, Frederick Vought, a Nazi scientist personally appointed by Adolf Hitler as chief physician at the Dachau concentration camp in 1939, conducted brutal and unethical human experimentation in pursuit of a serum designed to enhance human capabilities. His research ultimately led to the first successful iteration of Compound V, a prototype known as "V-One," which marked the beginning of what would later produce individuals known as "Supes."

With that used dosage of V, he created the first known superhero, his own wife, Klara Risinger, however, despite his success, Vought would never use Compound V to help the Nazi Party, since he realized that their defeat was certain. He soon defected from Nazi Germany and the Allied forces smuggled him and his wife to the United States, where he would give his newly developed serum to military soldiers to increase their odds against the Nazis (with Soldier Boy being the most notable example).

This prompted President Franklin Roosevelt to pardon him in 1944 and allow him to continue his research on Compound V, a decision that ultimately culminated in the formal incorporation of Vought-American in 1950, establishing the company as both a pharmaceutical manufacturer and defense R&D contractor dedicated to the continued research into Compound V and development of Supes as living weapons. However, it was in 1952 that the company underwent its first major corporate restructuring and public-facing brand expansion — an internal transformation that laid the groundwork for what would later be known as the "V52" era — marking the moment Vought shifted from a purely military enterprise into a broader cultural and media institution built around the mythology of superheroes.
